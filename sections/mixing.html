Compression
Foundation

HOW COMPRESSION WORKS

▸
Threshold: The level where compression kicks in. Lower = more compression engaged. Aim for -18 to -24dB on drums to get meaningful gain reduction.
▸
Ratio: How hard it clamps above threshold. 2:1 = gentle glue. 4:1 = moderate. 8:1+ = heavy. Limiting = ∞:1.
▸
Attack: How fast compression reacts. Slow attack (30–100ms) lets transients through for punch. Fast attack (1–5ms) tames peaks, good for controlling sibilance or harsh transients.
▸
Release: How fast compression lets go. Too fast = pumping. Too slow = squashed sustain. Match release to the tempo and groove of the track.
▸
Make-up gain: Compensate for gain reduction. Always gain-match before A/B testing — louder almost always sounds better regardless of quality.
Drums: 4:1–8:1
Bus: 2:1–4:1
Parallel crush: 8:1–∞
Use Cases

COMPRESSION TYPES

2:1
Glue, bus, gentle shaping
4:1
Drums, standard control
8:1
Aggressive, parallel crush
∞:1
Limiting, brick wall
▸
Serial: One compressor in the signal path. Simpler. Can kill transients if pushed too hard.
▸
Parallel: Compressed signal blended with dry. Preserves transients, adds body and sustain. Preferred for sampled drums.
▸
Sidechain: External signal triggers compression. Classic pump: kick triggers compression on bass or pad.
FL Studio / Settings

COMPRESSOR SETTINGS

▸
Fruity Compressor — preferred for drum bus. Simple, transparent. Use when you want clean gain reduction without coloration or multiband phase issues.
▸
Fruity Limiter (comp mode) — versatile. Set threshold low enough (~-18 to -24dB) to actually engage the compressor. The waveform display shows output level, not gain reduction — you can't read GR directly from it.
▸
kHs Compressor — part of the free Kilohearts Essentials pack. May have better visual feedback than Fruity options. Worth exploring for drum bus or pre-master work.
▸
Maximus — avoid on drum buses. Multiband introduces phase artifacts. Reserve for specific multiband tasks, not general compression.
▸
Judging GR without a meter: Bypass compressor and compensate with make-up gain until levels match. The difference between where the gain was vs. where you had to boost it back = roughly how much GR is happening.
EQ & Frequency
Reference

FREQUENCY SPECTRUM MAP

EQ Principles

GENERAL EQ RULES

▸
Cut before you boost. Removing problem frequencies makes room. Boosting on top of mud just makes more mud.
▸
High-pass everything that doesn't need sub. Pads, guitars, vocals — clean below 80Hz. Even tiny cuts clear meaningful headroom.
▸
Narrow cuts, wide boosts. Surgical narrow Q for problem frequencies. Wide shelves or gentle bells for adding character.
▸
Always EQ in context. Solo EQing is deceptive. What sounds right soloed often sounds wrong in the full mix.
▸
Don't over-boost. More than +4–6dB with a narrow bell usually means the sample needs replacing, not more EQ.
Drum Bus EQ

DRUM BUS EQ

▸
High-pass the Drum Crush track at 80–120Hz. The main bus handles the low end — the crush track only needs mids and up for body and energy.
▸
Tame harsh highs on the crush track (8–12kHz shelf cut). Heavily compressed hi-hats get brittle.
▸
Boost mids (1–3kHz) on the crush track for extra snare body and aggression to blend back in.
▸
High-pass reverb returns at 100–200Hz. Low-end reverb = mud, not space.
Mixing
Fundamentals

MIX FUNDAMENTALS

▸
Gain stage before anything else. Individual tracks peaking around -12 to -18dBFS. Headroom first, then mix.
▸
Mix in mono first. If it doesn't work in mono, stereo width is masking problems. Fix translation before adding width.
▸
Kick and bass are the foundation. Balance these two first. Everything else sits around them.
▸
Reference constantly. Your ears adjust to your own mix. Comparing against a commercial track recalibrates them.
▸
Take breaks. 20–30 min away resets ear fatigue. Decisions on fresh ears are almost always better.
Faders & Sends

FADER DISCIPLINE

▸
Blend with faders, not send amounts. Sends at 100%, use track faders for blend. Applies to parallel tracks and reverb/delay returns.
▸
Low-level monitoring test. If the mix still sounds balanced at low volume, the frequency balance is solid. Quiet = brutal truth-teller.
▸
Automation over static faders. Even subtle fader moves during transitions add energy and dimension a static mix can't have.
Reverb & Delay

REVERB & DELAY

▸
Send/return setup. Reverbs and delays on dedicated return tracks. Multiple elements feeding the same space = coherent room glue.
▸
High-pass reverb returns below 100–200Hz. Low-end reverb = mud.
▸
Pre-delay (10–25ms) separates the dry source from the reverb tail. Maintains clarity while keeping the effect present.
▸
Delay throws for variation. Automated delay on a single word or snare hit adds interest without a constant wet effect.
Stereo Field

PAN & WIDTH

▸
Kick and bass: center. Low frequencies lose energy and cause phase issues when panned.
▸
Hi-hats can be panned subtly (L or R 20–40%) to open up the center for main elements.
▸
Wide pads/chords fill the sides. If your melody is centered, widen chords to fill the stereo field.
▸
Haas effect (10–40ms delay on one side) creates width without pitch issues — but always check mono compatibility.

→
Limiter
▸
EQ (corrective): Fix frequency problems that made it through the mix. Small moves — 0.5 to 2dB max. More than that = a mix problem, not a mastering problem.
▸
Compressor (glue): 1.5:1–2:1, attack 50–100ms, release 200–400ms. 1–3dB GR max. Should be almost inaudible — you should only notice it when bypassed.
▸
Fruity Soft Clipper: Threshold -1 to -2dB. Rounds peaks so the limiter has less to catch. Post knob = compensate for level lost to clipping only. Use track fader (pre-master output) for final level control.
▸
Stereo width: Check/adjust stereo width. Mono the low end below ~150Hz. About translation, not creative decisions.
▸
Limiter ceiling: -1dBTP (true peak). 1–3dB of gain reduction at most. If it's working harder, something upstream needs fixing.
Pre-Master Chain

PRE-MASTER CHAIN

Bus Compressor
→
Saturation
▸
This is where heavier lifting happens. The pre-master does the work; the master tidies up. If the master is working hard, add more on the pre-master instead.
▸
Bus compressor: 3:1–4:1, attack 30–50ms, release 100–200ms. Aim for 3–6dB GR on the loudest parts. This is where the track starts to feel locked together.
▸
Saturation/color here, not on master. Character decisions belong on the pre-master. The master chain should be mostly corrective and transparent.
▸
Pre-master output: aim for peaks around -6 to -3dBFS going into the master chain.
Loudness & Translation

LOUDNESS TARGETS

Spotify: –14 LUFS
Apple: –16 LUFS
YouTube: –14 LUFS
▸
Mastering too loud backfires. Platforms normalize loudness. Over-limiting to hit –7 LUFS means your track gets turned down AND sounds squashed.
▸
Dynamic range goal: 6–10 DR for most modern beats. Trap often goes lower, acoustic goes higher.
▸
Check on multiple speakers. Laptop, headphones, earbuds, car. If it translates everywhere, it's done.
▸
Mastering can't fix mix problems. Muddy mix = muddy master. A limiter makes a bad mix loud and bad. Fix problems in the mix.
Signal Chain Reference
Saturation Tools

FL STUDIO SATURATION TOOLS

▸
kHs Clipper (Kilohearts Essentials — free) — a solid option for master saturation. Soft clipping adds harmonic color and rounds peaks simultaneously.
▸
Fruity Soft Clipper (native) — threshold + post makeup gain. Threshold near top for peak control, lower for character. Post = compensate for level lost, not to boost overall. Default -4.4dB is aggressive — start closer to -1dB on master.
▸
Fruity Blood Overdrive (native) — keep Color and Pre-amp low for subtle saturation. Best on individual tracks, works on master at very low settings.
▸
kHs Distortion (Kilohearts Essentials — free) — more aggressive, better for individual elements like 808s and leads. Try Saturate mode for musical results.
▸
Distructor (native) — full-featured distortion unit. More control than Blood Overdrive. Explore at low drive settings for character on individual tracks.
▸
Third-party options worth knowing: Softube Saturation Knob (free), Klanghelm SDRR (paid), Decapitator (paid) — all offer more refined saturation character than native FL tools for critical chain positions like the master.
Level Targets

GAIN STAGING TARGETS

▸
Individual tracks: peaks -12 to -18dBFS.
▸
Pre-master output: peaks -6 to -3dBFS into the master chain.
▸
Master output ceiling: -1dBTP true peak.
▸
Streaming targets: Spotify –14 LUFS, Apple –16 LUFS, YouTube –14 LUFS.
▸
Gain staging ≠ mixing. Setting healthy levels is the foundation. Actual mix balance happens with faders after gain staging is done.
⚠ Order matters throughout the chain. EQ before compression changes what gets compressed. Saturation before limiting gives the limiter a smoother signal. Always think about what each plugin is receiving, not just what it's doing.
