Drums & Sampling
Core Principle

SAMPLE SELECTION

▸
Start with better samples. Processing can shape a sound but can't manufacture weight or character. A great kick raw beats a bad kick compressed perfectly every time.
▸
Listen in context. A sample that sounds thin soloed might sit perfectly in a full mix. Always test before reaching for plugins.
▸
Layer sparingly. Two complementary kicks (one punchy, one low) beats stacking 5 mediocre ones. Each layer should serve a specific frequency role.
▸
Transient character matters. Snappy transient = cuts through a dense mix. Round/slow attack = blends in. Decide which you need before touching compression.
Groove & Feel

GROOVE & FEEL

▸
Velocity variation is everything. Ghost notes 30–50%, backbeats 60–80%, accents 85–100%. Flat velocity = robotic and lifeless.
▸
Micro-timing (swing). Nudging hi-hats or snares a few ticks late creates humanization. Use the Swing knob or shift notes manually in the Piano Roll.
▸
Space between hits. Removing a kick or snare hit creates tension that makes the next hit land harder. Less is often more.
▸
Hi-hat patterns drive energy. 16th note hats = high energy. 8th notes = relaxed. Open hats on upbeats = swing and bounce.
FL Studio Routing

DRUM BUS ROUTING

▸
Routing chain: Individual drum tracks → Drum Bus → Pre-Master. One fader controls all drums together.
▸
Send knobs stay at 100%. Use track faders to blend — not send amounts. Keeps signal structure clean and predictable.
▸
Parallel "Drum Crush" track: Separate mixer track receiving a send from the Drum Bus. Apply heavy compression, blend in with fader. Main bus transients stay intact.
▸
Parallel compression is preferable for sampled drums. Preserves the transients while adding body and sustain. Serial compression squashes the life out of samples.
⚠ Only build a drum bus when the project needs it. For simple beats, it adds unnecessary complexity.
Sound Selection
Choosing Sounds

PICKING THE RIGHT SOUND

▸
The sound should work dry. If it only works with heavy processing, it's the wrong sound. The right sound is already close before any plugins.
▸
Reference your target first. Listen to the track you're referencing, then search sounds while that sonic memory is fresh — not after 2 hours of browsing presets.
▸
Timbre serves the emotion. Bright/sharp = energetic/aggressive. Dark/soft = melancholic/introspective. Match the character of the sound to the mood before worrying about anything else.
▸
Less sustain = more rhythmic. Fast-decay sounds (plucks, stabs) work in dense rhythmic textures. Long sustain sounds (pads) work as harmonic beds. Know the role before searching.
▸
Never fall in love with a sound in solo. A sound that seems perfect soloed can clash badly in context. Always audition with the rest of the beat playing.
Synth Sounds

BUILDING FROM PRESETS

▸
Start from a preset, not from scratch. Find something in the right ballpark, then learn the parameters by ear. Faster than building from zero, and you learn the synth in real context.
▸
Melody matters more than the patch. The notes and rhythm you play define the character more than which preset you use. Don't spend an hour tweaking a synth if the melody isn't working.
▸
Detune for width and warmth. Unison voices with slight detuning (a few cents) adds thickness and movement. Too much = muddy. Just enough = full.
▸
Attack controls feel. Fast attack = punchy and upfront. Slow attack = soft entry, pad-like. Adjusting attack alone is often the quickest fix for how a synth sits in a mix.
▸
Release creates space or tightness. Short release = tight and dry. Long release = notes blur, atmospheric. Match release to the tempo and density of your track.
Layering

LAYERING SOUNDS

▸
Each layer needs a job. Don't layer sounds that do the same thing. One layer handles the sub frequencies, one handles the mids, one handles the high-end presence. Clear roles = coherent result.
▸
High-pass lower layers, low-pass upper layers. Remove the frequency ranges where layers overlap so they complement rather than compete. Frequency carving is what makes layering work.
▸
Tune layers to the same pitch. Layers even a few cents apart create beating and phasing that can destroy a sound when heard on different speakers.
▸
Less layers, more character. Two well-chosen, well-carved layers will almost always sound better than five poorly thought-out ones.
808s
Tuning & Selection

808 FUNDAMENTALS

▸
Tune your 808 to the key. An untuned 808 clashes with the melody even if it sounds "close enough." Use FL's pitch control to match the root note of your track.
▸
Pitch in context then saturate. Pitching shifts harmonic content, so any saturation should come after tuning — the harmonics change with pitch. In-context saturation is still valuable even on pre-processed commercial samples.
▸
Longer decay = more melodic. A long 808 acts like a bass instrument. Watch for clashes between the 808 pitch and your chord changes — it needs to be treated melodically, not like a drum hit.
▸
Sub feel vs. presence. Some 808s are mostly sub (felt physically). Others have more mid-range presence (heard on any speaker). You usually want both — use saturation to add mid presence to a sub-heavy sample.
Signal Chain

808 CHAIN

Multiband Enhancer
▸
Distortion/saturation first. Adds harmonics and upper-frequency content so the 808 translates on small speakers. kHs Distortion (free, Kilohearts Essentials) works well — try Saturate mode over Hard Clip for a more musical result on an 808.
▸
Soft Clipper second. Catches peaks created by the distortion. FL's native Fruity Soft Clipper default threshold of -4.4dB is aggressive — pull it up to -1 to -2dB for subtle control. Post knob compensates for lost level, but don't push it beyond your starting level. Use the track fader for overall 808 volume.
▸
Multiband enhancer last (optional). Soundgoodizer or a similar multiband presence tool polishes an already-shaped signal. Keep the mix knob well below 100% — most of these tools are heavy-handed at full wet. Skip it if the 808 already sounds right without it.
▸
Sidechain to kick. Kick ducks the 808 slightly each hit (3–5dB) so they share space. Even subtle ducking tightens everything up significantly.
Mixing

808 IN THE MIX

▸
Sub is felt, mid-bass is heard. Sub (40–80Hz) = physical weight. Mid-bass (80–200Hz) = presence on small speakers. You need both to translate everywhere.
▸
Keep low end mono. Stereo sub frequencies cause phase cancellation on mono systems (earbuds, phones, clubs). Use a stereo imager plugin to mono below ~150Hz. FL's native Fruity Stereo Shaper or kHs Stereo (free, Kilohearts Essentials) both work well for this.
▸
Check on small speakers. If your 808 disappears on earbuds, the sub is doing all the work. Add saturation or a mid-bass layer.
▸
Watch for clashes with melody. A long, sustaining 808 that doesn't match the chord changes will make the track sound out of tune. Either shorten the decay or pitch the 808 per bar.
Melodies
Construction

WRITING MELODIES

▸
Call and response. Write a 2-bar phrase, then answer it with a 2-bar phrase. The oldest melodic structure that exists — it works every time.
▸
Repetition first, variation second. Establish a motif (even just 3–4 notes), repeat it, then vary it. The ear needs to recognize something before it can appreciate change.
▸
Rhythm is half the melody. The same 4 notes in different rhythmic patterns = completely different melodies. Experiment with note length and placement before changing pitches.
▸
Stay mostly stepwise. Move by 2nds and 3rds most of the time. Leaps (4ths, 5ths, octaves) are powerful — save them for moments of emphasis.
▸
Land on strong beats. Place important notes on beat 1 or 3 to create resolve. Tension on beat 2 and 4 creates anticipation.
▸
Hum it first. If you can hum the melody before playing it, it's probably memorable. If you can't, your listener won't be able to either.
Feel & Character

MAKING IT INTERESTING

▸
Silence is part of the melody. Gaps and rests give the listener room to breathe and anticipate the next phrase. Don't fill every bar.
▸
Pentatonic is forgiving. Major pentatonic (1 2 3 5 6) and minor pentatonic (1 b3 4 5 b7) sound musical in almost any context. Great starting point when stuck.
▸
Pitch bends and slides. A note bent up to pitch or slid between notes adds expression. In FL: use the pitch envelope or draw bends in the Piano Roll using the pitch control lane.
▸
Countermelody. A second, simpler melody that moves in opposition (when main goes up, counter goes down) adds depth without clutter.
▸
Chromatic approach notes. Play a note one semitone below your target right before landing on it. Creates a smooth "slide into" effect even on a keyboard instrument.
Production

MELODY IN THE MIX

▸
One lead, everything else supporting. If two melodies are fighting for attention, one needs to drop in volume, frequency range, or rhythmic activity.
▸
Octave doubling. Duplicate the melody up or down an octave, blend it low in the mix. Adds thickness without competing for the same sonic space.
▸
Unison with a different instrument. Same notes, different timbre — a piano melody doubled by a soft synth pad creates a hybrid sound neither instrument has alone.
▸
Humanize MIDI velocity. Even small velocity differences (±10–15 on random notes) make a programmed melody feel played, not typed.
Harmonizing Melodies
The Concept

WHY 3RDS & 6THS WORK

▸
Harmonizing = doubling the melody at a fixed interval. You play the exact same rhythm as the lead, but every note is shifted up or down by a consistent interval — usually a 3rd or 6th.
▸
3rds are the most common harmony interval. A note stacked a 3rd above (or below) the lead is consonant but adds color — not as hollow as a 5th, not as thick as a unison. Most of the harmony you hear in music is built on 3rds.
▸
6ths are the inverse of 3rds. A harmony a 6th above is the same as a 3rd below — just in a different octave. 6ths tend to sound a little sweeter and more open than 3rds stacked on top. Both are valid, just different flavors.
▸
Octaves = thickness without color. Doubling a melody an octave up or down makes it bigger but doesn't add harmonic complexity. Useful for reinforcing, not for creating a lush harmony sound.
▸
5ths = powerful but hollow. Stacking a 5th above sounds huge and open, but has no major/minor character. More useful for bass lines and power chord situations than melodic harmonies.
Diatonic vs Parallel

TWO APPROACHES

▸
Diatonic harmony (stay in key). Every harmony note stays within the scale of the track. The interval between the lead and harmony shifts slightly from note to note (sometimes a minor 3rd, sometimes a major 3rd) but everything remains in key. This is the most musical-sounding approach and what most harmony naturally is.
▸
Parallel harmony (fixed interval). The interval between lead and harmony stays exactly the same for every note — always a major 3rd, always a minor 3rd, etc. Some notes will fall outside the key. Sounds slightly alien or tense, which can be intentional and effective in melodic trap.
▸
How to find diatonic 3rds in practice: In the Piano Roll, select all your lead notes. Copy them to a new instrument track. Then shift every note up by 2 scale steps (not 2 semitones — 2 steps within the key). In Am, if the lead is A, the diatonic 3rd above is C. If the lead is E, the 3rd is G. The interval varies but all notes stay in key.
▸
Quick rule for minor keys: Starting from any note in the scale, count up 2 white keys (on a piano) to find the diatonic 3rd. This keeps you in key automatically without having to think about it theoretically.
In the Mix

USING HARMONIES IN PRODUCTION

▸
Harmony should support, not compete. Keep the harmony layer lower in volume than the lead — maybe 4–8dB quieter. It should add fullness and warmth without the listener consciously noticing it's a separate note.
▸
Use a different sound for the harmony. If the lead is a bright pluck, use a softer, darker version of the same sound (or a completely different instrument) for the harmony. Keeps the lead cutting through while the harmony fills space beneath it.
▸
Pan the harmony off-center. Lead in the center, harmony panned slightly L or R (20–40%). Creates width and separation so the two voices occupy different space in the stereo field.
▸
More reverb on the harmony. Pushing the harmony further back in the mix with extra reverb makes it feel like it's supporting the lead rather than sitting alongside it as an equal.
▸
Don't harmonize every bar. Let the lead play alone for a bar or two, then bring in the harmony for the second half of the phrase. The entrance of the harmony becomes a moment — an arrival — instead of just a constant texture.
▸
Harmony vs. countermelody. A harmony follows the lead note-for-note at a fixed interval. A countermelody moves independently — different rhythm, different contour, sometimes going the opposite direction. Both are useful, but they're different tools. Harmonies add thickness; countermelodies add complexity.
FL Studio

DOING IT IN FL

▸
Duplicate the lead pattern. Right-click the pattern block in the Song view and duplicate it, or copy the Piano Roll contents to a new instrument channel. This is your harmony layer starting point.
▸
Select all notes, then shift up by interval. In the Piano Roll, Ctrl+A to select all, then use Shift+Up Arrow to move notes up by semitone. A diatonic 3rd in a minor key is usually 3 or 4 semitones depending on the note — you'll need to correct individual notes to stay in key for diatonic harmony.
▸
Easier approach: use a different instrument. Keep the lead on one channel, add the harmony notes manually on a second channel using a softer/darker patch. More work upfront but gives you more control over each note's velocity and timing independently.
▸
Stack both on the same mixer track. Route the lead and harmony to the same mixer insert so they share the same reverb, delay, and EQ. This makes them feel like one cohesive sound rather than two separate elements fighting each other.
Basslines
Foundation

WRITING BASSLINES

▸
Lock with the kick. The most important relationship in a beat. Kick hits on beat 1, bass root usually lands there too. Shared hits = unity; offsets = groove.
▸
Root notes first, then movement. Get the harmonic foundation right (roots of your chords on downbeats), then add passing notes and fills between them.
▸
Note length matters. Short/staccato = tight and punchy. Long/sustained = warm and flowing. Match the energy of the track.
▸
The octave jump. Play the root, then jump up an octave mid-phrase. Classic hip-hop/funk move that adds bounce without complexity.
▸
Anticipation (push). Play the bass note a 16th note before the downbeat of the next chord. Creates forward momentum and groove.
Mixing

BASS IN THE MIX

▸
Sub vs. mid bass. Sub (40–80Hz) is felt. Mid-bass (80–200Hz) is heard. Layer both — sub for weight, mids for presence on small speakers.
▸
Sidechain bass to kick. Even 3–5dB of ducking when the kick hits gives both elements room. Tightens the low end significantly.
▸
Keep bass mono below ~150Hz. Stereo sub causes phase cancellation on mono systems. Use a stereo imager to mono the low end.
▸
Saturation adds harmonics. A pure sine sub is invisible on small speakers. Subtle saturation creates upper harmonics that represent the note on any playback system.
▸
Check in mono at low volume. If your bass disappears, the sub is doing all the work. Add a mid-bass layer or more saturation.
Arpeggios
Construction

WRITING ARPS

▸
An arp is a chord played one note at a time. Start by building the chord, then decide the order and rhythm. Up, down, up-down, random — each creates a completely different feel.
▸
Note length defines character. Short staccato arps = driving and rhythmic. Longer overlapping notes = flowing and atmospheric. Try both on the same pattern.
▸
Extend across octaves. Play chord tones in one octave, then continue the pattern up into the next octave before coming back down. Creates a bigger, sweeping arc.
▸
Rhythmic placement creates groove. Don't just play straight 16th note arps. Skip beats, add rests, accent certain notes. The rhythm of the arp is as important as the notes.
▸
Add non-chord passing tones. Weave in scale notes between chord tones. Stops the arp feeling mechanical and adds melodic interest.
▸
Lock arp rhythm to the hi-hat. If hats are 16th notes, try arping on every other 16th. Rhythmic contrast creates interplay instead of cluttered repetition.
Production

ARPS IN THE MIX

▸
Arps work best as texture, not lead. Unless the arp IS the main hook, keep it supporting. Pull the volume back, add reverb, let it sit behind the lead melody.
▸
Use a different sound than your lead. If the melody is a bright pluck, make the arp a softer pad or bell so they occupy different tonal space.
▸
Pan arps slightly off-center. 20–30% L or R separates the arp from a centered lead and gives it its own place in the stereo field.
▸
Synced delay on an arp. A 1/8 or 1/16 note ping-pong delay creates the illusion of a wider, more complex pattern from a simple one. The delay becomes part of the rhythm.
▸
Automate the filter cutoff. Slowly opening or closing a low-pass filter over the arp creates movement and evolution without changing a single note.
▸
Velocity shape the arp. Accent every 4th or 8th note at higher velocity. Creates a secondary rhythm inside the pattern that adds groove.
FL Studio

ARP TOOLS IN FL

▸
Draw arps manually in Piano Roll. Most control. Use the Stamp tool to drop a chord shape, then manually stagger the notes in time.
▸
FL's Arpeggiator. Found on some instruments (instrument settings → Arp/Chord tab). Auto-generates patterns from held chords. Good for exploring ideas quickly without programming by hand.
▸
Chord stamp tool. Stamp chord, rearrange notes into arp rhythm. Faster than placing notes one by one when you already know the chord voicing.
Chord Progressions
Progressions That Work

GO-TO PROGRESSIONS

▸
i – VII – VI – VII (minor loop) — Dark, circular, hypnotic. Foundation of a huge amount of trap and hip-hop. In Am: Am – G – F – G.
▸
I – V – vi – IV (major) — Uplifting and resolved. Ubiquitous for a reason. In C: C – G – Am – F.
▸
i – VI – III – VII — Minor but melodic and melancholic. More emotional feel. In Am: Am – F – C – G.
▸
Two-chord loops. Don't underestimate them. i – VII on repeat is hypnotic and works endlessly in sample-based music. Simplicity beats overcrowded harmony.
▸
Modal borrowing. Swap one chord from the parallel minor into a major key for a surprising color change. In C major, adding a bVII (Bb) chord creates a cinematic shift.
▸
iv vs IV in a minor key. iv (minor four) sounds somber. IV (major four) in a minor key sounds surprisingly bright and emotional. Try both — they're very different.
▸
Sus2 and sus4 chords. No 3rd = harmonically ambiguous. Great for pads that sit under anything without clashing with the melody's major/minor character.
▸
Relative major/minor. Am and C major share the same notes. You can freely borrow chords between them — same key, different tonal center.
Voicing

HOW TO VOICE CHORDS

▸
Inversions add smoothness. Instead of always root position, try 1st or 2nd inversion so the top note moves stepwise between chords rather than jumping. This is called voice leading — it's what separates pro-sounding chords from amateur ones.
▸
Spread wide voicings. Separate chord tones across octaves (root in bass, 5th in mid, 3rd high up) for an open, atmospheric sound.
▸
Add 7ths and 9ths. maj7 = dreamy. min7 = smooth. add9 = modern and open. Extensions lift a basic triad into something more interesting.
▸
Strum vs. block vs. arpeggio. Block = all notes at once, hits hard. Arpeggiated = notes one at a time, lighter feel. Strum pattern = groove. Same chord, three completely different feels.
Scales Reference

SCALES TO KNOW

▸
Natural minor (Aeolian): 1 2 b3 4 5 b6 b7 — Default dark/emotional. Fits most hip-hop and trap.
▸
Dorian mode: 1 2 b3 4 5 6 b7 — Minor with raised 6th. Cool, sophisticated, soulful. Common in neo-soul and jazz-influenced production.
▸
Phrygian: 1 b2 b3 4 5 b6 b7 — The b2 gives it a dark, tense feel. Great for moody, menacing beats.
▸
Lydian: 1 2 3 #4 5 6 7 — Major with raised 4th. Dreamy, floating, cinematic.
▸
Minor pentatonic: 1 b3 4 5 b7 — Five notes, almost always sounds good. Safe zone when melody writing feels stuck.

Musical Layering
The Concept

HOW LAYERS BUILD A BEAT

A complete beat is built from multiple musical layers that each serve a different role — rhythmic, harmonic, melodic, textural. The art is knowing which layers to combine, how to make them share space without competing, and when to add or remove them for arrangement interest. Below are the main layer types and how they interact.

▸
Rhythm layer (drums). The foundation. Sets the tempo, groove, and energy level. Everything else locks to this. Without a strong rhythmic foundation, no amount of melodic layering will save the beat.
▸
Bass layer (808/bassline). Bridges rhythm and harmony — it's both rhythmic and pitched. Locks to the kick rhythmically and locks to the key harmonically. The low-end relationship between the kick and bass defines the pocket of the beat.
▸
Harmonic bed (pads/chords). The harmonic context that everything else sits on top of. Usually sustained or slow-moving. Doesn't compete with the melody — it supports it by defining the chord underneath.
▸
Lead melody. The main musical idea — the hook the listener remembers. Should be the clearest, most upfront melodic element. Everything else moves out of the way for this.
▸
Counter-melody / harmony. A secondary melodic line that supports the lead without copying it. Either moves in opposition (countermelody) or doubles at a fixed interval like a 3rd or 6th (harmony). Adds richness without adding a new focal point.
▸
Arpeggio layer. A chord played note-by-note in a rhythmic pattern. Adds movement and interest to a chord that might otherwise feel static. Works well as a background texture when kept lower in volume than the lead.
▸
Textural layer. Ambient sounds, vinyl crackle, noise, reversed hits, processed samples, atmospheric pads. No defined melodic content — purely about vibe, space, and glue. The layer most producers forget and most listeners feel without noticing.
▸
Ear candy / stabs. Short, rhythmic hits — chord stabs, single notes, vocal chops, percussion hits placed between the main beats. Add excitement and movement without adding harmonic complexity.
Common Combinations

LAYER COMBINATIONS THAT WORK

▸
Drums + 808 + Lead melody. The minimal trap setup. Rhythm, bass, and one focal point. Everything stripped to essentials — forces the melody to carry the track. Used constantly in emo-rap where the vibe matters more than complexity.
▸
Drums + 808 + Chords + Lead. Add a pad or chord progression under the melody. The chords define the harmony so the lead can focus on the melodic line rather than implying the full chord by itself. Standard setup for most melodic trap.
▸
Drums + 808 + Chords + Arp + Lead. The arp adds rhythmic movement to the harmonic layer without adding a new melodic idea. Keep the arp lower in volume than the lead and in a different frequency range — bright arp under a darker lead, or a softer arp under a brighter lead.
▸
Drums + 808 + Chords + Lead + Harmony. Double the lead at a 3rd or 6th interval, lower in volume and pushed back with reverb. The lead stays clear and present while the harmony adds width and emotional weight. One of the most effective combinations in melodic production.
▸
Drums + 808 + Pad + Lead + Counter-melody. A second melodic line that moves independently of the lead — different rhythm, different contour. Creates conversation between two melodic ideas. Works when the main melody has space (rests, gaps) for the counter-melody to fill.
▸
Drums + 808 + Chords + Arp + Lead + Harmony + Texture. The full layered setup. Each element has a clear role and frequency zone. This is where arrangement becomes crucial — not everything should play all the time. Strip elements back in verses, bring them all in for the hook.
Making Layers Work Together

LAYER INTERACTION RULES

▸
Different rhythms = less competition. If your lead melody and arp are both playing on the same beats, they blur together. Offset them — when the melody holds a long note, the arp moves. When the arp rests, the melody fills.
▸
Different frequency zones = clarity. Lead in the upper-mids, chords in the mids, arp in the high-mids, pad in the lower-mids, bass in the low end. When layers occupy different frequency zones they can all be heard simultaneously without fighting.
▸
Different stereo positions = width. Lead center, chords wide, arp slightly panned, pad very wide, bass mono. Each layer occupying a different stereo position creates a full, three-dimensional sound without muddiness.
▸
Different reverb depths = front-to-back. Lead dry or short reverb (upfront), arp medium reverb (mid-distance), pad heavy reverb (far back), texture very wet (background). Layering reverb depth creates physical depth in the mix.
▸
Volume hierarchy. Lead at unity, harmony -4 to -8dB below lead, arp -8 to -12dB below lead, chords/pads -10 to -14dB below lead, texture barely audible. If any supporting layer is as loud as the lead, it's competing rather than supporting.
▸
Build through the arrangement. Don't start with all layers — introduce them progressively. Drums and bass first, then the main melody, then chords, then the arp, then the harmony. Each new layer is a moment of interest and arrival for the listener.
Ideas to Try

LAYERING IDEAS TO EXPERIMENT WITH

▸
Chord stabs as rhythm. Instead of a sustained chord pad, use short staccato chord hits on specific beats. The chord becomes rhythmic — part harmony, part percussion. Works well on the off-beats or in syncopated patterns.
▸
Arp that outlines the chord. Make the arp play the notes of each chord in sequence as the progression moves. The arp becomes a moving harmonic guide — the listener hears the chord change through the arp even if the pad is subtle.
▸
Melody doubled by a bell one octave up. The main melody on a synth, doubled note-for-note by a bell or pluck an octave higher at -6 to -8dB. The bell adds shimmer and attack without a separate melodic idea.
▸
Sub pad under everything. A very low, slow-moving pad (near the bottom of the audible range, 40–80Hz) that follows the root notes of the chord progression. Felt more than heard. Adds physical weight and warmth to the low end without interfering with the 808.
▸
Vocal chop as a melodic layer. A chopped, pitched vocal sample that plays along with the chord progression — not as a lead melody but as a textural, rhythmic harmonic element. Adds a human quality that synthesizers can't fully replicate.
▸
Noise layer with filter automation. A filtered noise or texture that sweeps from dark to bright across a section. Not a musical element — purely textural. Creates anticipation and movement without adding any notes or competing with the harmony.
Sample Flipping & Chopping
The Concept

WHAT SAMPLE FLIPPING IS

▸
Taking an existing recording and making it new. A soul record, a jazz loop, a film score, a vocal acapella — anything. Sample flipping means chopping it into pieces, pitching and rearranging those pieces, and building a beat around what's left. The source material becomes raw material, not the finished product.
▸
Why it's useful: instant sonic grounding. A real recording carries room tone, tape/vinyl character, performance imperfections, and harmonic complexity that synthesizers struggle to replicate convincingly. Flipping a sample instantly puts a beat in a real sonic world instead of a purely digital one.
▸
Why it's useful: breaks creative block. Starting from nothing (a blank Piano Roll) is intimidating. Starting by reacting to something that already exists — what does this chord hit want to do next, what happens if I reverse this phrase — is a completely different, often faster creative process.
Workflow

HOW TO CHOP A SAMPLE IN FL STUDIO

▸
Get the sample into FL. Drag the audio file into the Playlist as an audio clip, or into the Channel Rack as a Sampler channel. Either way, you now have the full, unchopped recording loaded and ready to edit.
▸
Open it in an editor and find the moments. Double-click the clip to open it in the audio clip editor, or drop it into Edison for more detailed editing. Listen through and mark the interesting moments — a chord hit, a vocal phrase, a single drum break, one isolated note.
▸
Slice it. Use Fruity Slicer to automatically chop the sample into slices mapped across the keyboard (each slice triggered by a different note), or slice manually in Edison and drag each region out to its own Sampler channel. Either approach turns one long file into individually playable pieces.
▸
Rearrange and repitch in the Piano Roll. With slices mapped to notes, you can now play them in any order, any rhythm, at any pitch — just like a normal instrument. Pitching a non-melodic slice (a stab, a hit, a word) up and down across different notes is how producers turn a single chopped sound into a full melody.
Technique

MAKING IT SOUND ORIGINAL

▸
Reverse slices. Playing a chopped piece backward changes its envelope entirely — a percussive hit becomes a swell. Instantly less recognizable than the source.
▸
Pitch the whole sample 3–7 semitones. Shifting the entire sample's key and timbre this far changes the vocal/instrument character noticeably — a deep male vocal can read as androgynous or alien, a bright horn can read as dark and warm.
▸
Time-stretch individual slices. Stretching a slice without changing its pitch (or letting pitch shift as a side effect) changes its texture and timing relationship to the original — useful for fitting a slice to your tempo or creating an unnatural, warped character.
▸
Saturate and filter. Adding saturation, distortion, or aggressive EQ filtering changes the texture enough that the source becomes harder to place. A bright, clean sample run through heavy saturation and a low-pass filter can sound like an entirely different recording.
▸
Layer your own drums and bass underneath. A sample flip rarely stands alone — your own 808, kick, and hi-hats on top of the chopped material is what makes it sound like your beat rather than someone else's record with drums added.
▸
Chop one or two notes, not the whole phrase. Instead of looping a full recognizable phrase, take a single note or hit from the source and use it as one melodic element among many — built into a new progression rather than carrying the original's melody.
The goal isn't to use a sample — it's to take something and make it yours.
Sources

WHERE TO FIND SAMPLES

▸
Vinyl records. The classic approach — digging through used record stores or thrift shops for soul, jazz, funk, and obscure recordings nobody else has flipped yet. Requires a turntable and an audio interface to record into FL.
▸
YouTube. A massive archive of old soul, jazz, and R&B recordings, live performances, and obscure foreign-language records. Useful for finding material, though anything pulled this way is just as uncleared as a vinyl rip.
▸
Free sample sites. Looperman and Free Music Archive both host loops and recordings that are free to use, with usage terms attached to each upload — check the specific license on what you download.
▸
Sample packs built for chopping. Many commercial and free sample packs include full loops (not just one-shots) specifically intended to be sliced and rearranged rather than used whole.
▸
Splice. A subscription sample library with royalty-free loops, one-shots, and full vocal/instrumental stems licensed for use in commercial releases without separate clearance.
⚠ Copyright: an uncleared sample from a commercial recording carries real legal risk if you release the music commercially — for practice and learning, it doesn't matter. Clearing a sample means getting permission from the rights holders (the recording owner and the songwriting/publishing owner), which is often expensive and complex. Many producers stick to royalty-free sources instead, or try to flip a sample heavily enough that it arguably becomes a new work — but that line is legally ambiguous, not a guaranteed defense.
