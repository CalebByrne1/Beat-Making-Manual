Common Mistakes
Drums

DRUM MISTAKES

▸
Flat velocity on everything. Programming every hit at the same velocity (usually 100) makes drums sound robotic. Real drums are never even — ghost notes are quiet, accents are loud. Add velocity variation to every pattern.
▸
Too many layers on the kick. Stacking 4–5 kicks on top of each other doesn't make a bigger kick — it usually makes a phasey, unclear mess. One great sample > four mediocre ones. Layer only when each layer has a specific job.
▸
Kick and 808 fighting each other. If the kick and 808 both have lots of energy in the 60–120Hz range, they'll cancel each other out or sound muddy. Sidechain the 808 to the kick, or EQ one to make room for the other.
▸
No variation in 8 bars. A drum pattern that loops identically for 16+ bars loses the listener. Even small changes — a ghost note, a removed kick, a drum fill on bar 8 — keep the groove interesting.
▸
Over-compressing the drum bus. Slamming the drum bus compressor until the transients disappear kills the punch that makes trap and hip-hop drums hit hard. Use parallel compression — heavy compression on the crush track, blended in, while the dry transients stay intact.
Mixing

MIXING MISTAKES

▸
Mixing at too high a volume. Ear fatigue sets in fast at loud volumes and your perception of low frequencies exaggerates. Mix at a comfortable conversation volume for most of the session. Only check at higher volumes briefly.
▸
Never checking in mono. Stereo width can hide phase problems and frequency clashes. Flip to mono regularly — if elements disappear or the mix sounds drastically different, there are problems that width was covering up.
▸
Too much reverb on everything. When everything has reverb, nothing sounds like it's in a real space — it just sounds washed out. Reserve heavy reverb for specific elements (pads, background elements, effect throws). Keep leads and drums relatively dry.
▸
EQing in solo. A frequency that sounds perfect on a soloed synth can clash horribly in the full mix. Always make EQ decisions in context. Solo briefly to identify a problem, then fix it while listening to the full mix.
▸
Chasing loudness too early. Pushing the master limiter hard before the mix is balanced just makes a bad mix loud. Get the mix right first. Loudness comes last — and modern streaming platforms normalize it anyway.
▸
Untuned 808. An 808 even slightly out of tune with the melody clashes immediately and sounds amateur. Always tune the 808 to the root note of the key before anything else. This single fix will improve your beats more than any plugin.
Arrangement

ARRANGEMENT MISTAKES

▸
Everything the same energy from start to finish. A beat that stays at full density for 3 minutes is exhausting and boring. Energy needs to move — build up, back off, build again. Map your energy before arranging.
▸
Intro that takes too long. An 8-bar drums-only intro before the melody comes in might work for a vinyl DJ — for streaming it loses listeners. Get to the hook by bar 8–16 maximum. People decide fast.
▸
No transitions between sections. Cutting directly from a verse to a hook without any signal (riser, fill, stutter, filter sweep) sounds abrupt and unfinished. Even a reversed snare on the last beat makes a huge difference.
▸
Verses and hooks that sound the same. If there's no dynamic difference between your verse and hook, the hook doesn't pay off. Either add elements to the hook or subtract from the verse — or both.
▸
Building one loop forever. Looping an 8-bar pattern and adding effects for 30 minutes before arranging is how beats stay unfinished. Sketch the full structure early — even rough — then fill it in.
Production

GENERAL MISTAKES

▸
Chasing the perfect sound before finishing. Spending an hour on a synth patch before writing a single note is procrastination dressed as production. Get a placeholder sound, write the melody, then refine the patch. Music first, sound design second.
▸
Adding more plugins instead of fixing the problem. Stacking three EQs, a compressor, and two saturators on a sound that just isn't working rarely fixes it. The right sample or patch, chosen well, needs minimal processing. Stop adding and start replacing.
▸
Working on the same beat for too long. After 3–4 hours on one beat, your perspective is gone. You'll make bad decisions and over-process things that were already fine. Export what you have, start something new, come back tomorrow.
▸
Not referencing. Working without a reference track is like painting in the dark. Load a commercial track you're aiming toward into FL, match its volume with SPAN, and compare regularly. Without a reference, you have no idea how far off you are.
▸
Never finishing anything. The most common mistake of all. Fifty unfinished beats teach you less than five finished ones. Finishing forces you to solve problems you'd otherwise abandon. Build the habit of completing things, even when they're not perfect.
Sound Design

WHY YOUR BEAT SOUNDS LIKE A VIDEO GAME

▸
Bright, clean synth patches with fast attacks. Default saw/square patches with an instant attack and no filtering read as "8-bit soundtrack," not record. Fix: round off the attack slightly and add a low-pass filter or light saturation to take the clean digital edge off.
▸
Melodies that only move upward. A line that just keeps climbing with no falls or held tension feels like a level-up jingle. Fix: let the melody come back down, hold a note, leave a gap — real phrases breathe in both directions.
▸
No low-end presence. Without a sub bass or 808 anchoring the bottom, a beat feels thin and "in-game" instead of weighty. Fix: add a simple sub layer under the melody — it grounds the sound immediately.
▸
No space in the arrangement. Constant melodic motion in every bar with no rests reads as background music, not a song built around a hook. Fix: cut elements out for a few bars at a time and let drums or bass carry a section alone — see Creating Space.
Glossary

Quick definitions for terms used throughout this manual and in production communities. If you hear a word you don't know, check here first.

A – G

GLOSSARY A–G

ADSR
Attack, Decay, Sustain, Release — the four stages of a synth envelope that control how a note evolves over time.
Arpeggio
A chord played one note at a time in sequence rather than all at once.
Attack
The initial transient of a sound, or the compressor parameter that controls how fast it reacts to a signal above the threshold.
Automation
Programming a parameter (volume, filter, reverb wet) to change over time automatically during playback.
BPM
Beats Per Minute — the tempo of a track. Trap typically ranges from 130–160 BPM.
Bus
A mixer track that receives signals from multiple other tracks. A drum bus collects all drum tracks so they can be processed together.
Choke
When one sample stops (chokes) another when triggered. Commonly used so an open hi-hat gets cut off when a closed hi-hat plays — like a real hi-hat cymbal.
Clipping
When audio exceeds 0dBFS, causing the waveform to be cut flat and producing harsh distortion. Hard clipping is unwanted; soft clipping can add saturation.
dBFS
Decibels Full Scale — the unit of volume measurement in a DAW. 0dBFS is the maximum before clipping.
DAW
Digital Audio Workstation — software used to produce, record, and mix music. FL Studio is a DAW.
Decay
The stage after the initial attack where a sound falls from its peak level to the sustain level. Affects how long the initial transient rings.
De-essing
Reducing harsh sibilant frequencies (the "s" and "sh" sounds) in a vocal track using a frequency-specific compressor called a de-esser.
Diatonic
Notes or chords that belong to a particular key or scale. Diatonic harmony stays within the key of the track.
Dry
The original, unprocessed signal. Opposite of wet.
Dynamic Range
The difference between the quietest and loudest parts of a track. Higher dynamic range = more variation in volume. Heavy limiting reduces dynamic range.
EQ
Equalizer — a tool for boosting or cutting specific frequencies in an audio signal. Used to shape tone, remove problems, and carve space in a mix.
Filter
Removes frequencies above (low-pass), below (high-pass), or outside a range (band-pass) of a set cutoff frequency. Used in synthesis and mixing.
Gain
The amplification of an audio signal. Increasing gain = louder. Used at multiple points in the chain to control signal levels.
Ghost Note
A very quiet drum hit (typically a snare or hi-hat) placed between main hits to add subtle texture and humanization to a groove. Usually 30–50%.
Glue
When compression or saturation makes separate elements feel like they belong together as one cohesive sound. "Glue compression" on a bus creates this effect.
H – P

GLOSSARY H–P

Harmonics
Frequencies that are whole-number multiples of a fundamental frequency. Saturation and distortion add harmonics, which is why they make sounds fuller and more audible on small speakers.
Headroom
The space between your current peak level and 0dBFS. Headroom gives processing room to work without pushing into clipping.
High-Pass Filter
Removes frequencies below a set cutoff point, allowing high frequencies to pass through. Used to clean low-end rumble from non-bass elements.
Insert Effect
A plugin placed directly in a track's signal chain, processing the full signal serially. Every plugin in FL's mixer insert slot is an insert effect.
Key (Music)
The tonal center of a piece of music — the scale and root note everything is built around. A track in A minor uses notes from the A minor scale.
LFO
Low Frequency Oscillator — a modulation source that moves a parameter (filter, pitch, volume) up and down over time, creating movement and animation in a sound.
Limiter
A compressor with an extremely high ratio (∞:1) that prevents audio from exceeding a set ceiling. Used as the final stage in the master chain.
LUFS
Loudness Units relative to Full Scale — a loudness measurement that accounts for how humans perceive volume over time. Used by streaming platforms for normalization. Spotify targets –14 LUFS.
MIDI
Musical Instrument Digital Interface — data that tells a virtual instrument what notes to play, when, and how loud. Not audio — it's instructions that an instrument interprets.
Mix Bus
The master mixer channel that receives all other tracks. In FL Studio this is the Master insert. The final output before export.
Modal Borrowing
Using a chord from the parallel major or minor key within a progression. E.g., using a bVII chord from the parallel minor in a major key for an unexpected color shift.
Mono
A single channel of audio that sounds the same from all speakers. Opposite of stereo. Important reference point for checking mix translation.
Oscillator
The sound source in a synthesizer that generates the raw waveform. Common waveforms: sine (smooth), saw (bright/buzzy), square (hollow), triangle (soft).
Parallel
Processing where the original dry signal runs alongside a processed copy. Both are blended together. Preserves the original character while adding the effect.
Peak Controller
An FL Studio native plugin that reads the peak level of an audio signal and uses it to control any other parameter — volume faders, filter cutoffs, etc. Great for sidechain without a compressor.
Pentatonic
A 5-note scale (penta = five). Minor pentatonic (1 b3 4 5 b7) and major pentatonic (1 2 3 5 6) are the most common starting points for melody writing — nearly every note sounds good together.
R – Z

GLOSSARY R–Z

Release
In synthesis: how long a sound rings out after a key is released. In compression: how fast the compressor stops compressing after the signal drops below the threshold.
Resample
Recording the output of your DAW (or a section of it) as a new audio file, then using that recording as a sample. Useful for creating new textures from your own work.
Reverb
Simulates the natural reflections of sound in a physical space. Adds depth and pushes elements back in the mix. Long reverb = big, spacious. Short reverb = intimate.
Saturation
Soft distortion that adds harmonic content to a signal, making it warmer, louder-sounding, and more audible on small speakers. Comes from analog tape and tube amplifier emulation.
Send
A copy of a signal routed to a separate mixer return track (reverb, delay, parallel compression) without interrupting the original dry signal path.
Sidechain
Using one audio signal to trigger processing on another. The classic example: a kick drum sidechaining a compressor on the bass, ducking the bass every time the kick hits.
Signal Chain
The path audio takes from its source through all processing stages to the output. Understanding signal chain means understanding what is happening to audio at every step.
Stereo
Two channels of audio (left and right) that create a sense of width and space. Stereo content needs to also sound good in mono for translation across all playback systems.
Sustain
The level a sound holds while a key is held after the decay phase. High sustain = long-held notes. Low sustain = the sound quickly fades to silence while held.
Swing / Shuffle
Rhythmic feel created by slightly delaying off-beat notes. Adds a human, groove-based feel to programmed drum patterns. In FL Studio, adjusted via the Swing knob or by nudging notes manually.
Threshold
The level at which a compressor, limiter, or gate begins to act on the signal. Below the threshold = no processing. Above it = the plugin engages.
Transient
The sharp initial spike at the start of a sound — the "crack" of a snare, the "click" of a kick, the "pluck" of a guitar. Transients define punch and clarity.
Velocity
In MIDI: how hard a note is hit, expressed as a value from 1–127. Higher velocity = louder and often brighter. Varying velocity is crucial for making programmed patterns feel human.
Wet
The processed or effected version of a signal — the reverb tail, the delay repeat, the compressed output. Most processing blends wet with dry.
Wavetable
A synthesis method where the oscillator cycles through a table of stored waveforms. Scanning through the wavetable creates evolving, animated timbres. Used extensively in popular synths like Vital (free) and Serum.

120–128 BPM
Four-on-the-floor
Major keys
▸

▸

How To Use This

FINDING YOUR LANE

These maps aren't rules — they're starting points. Most great records borrow across lanes: a melodic trap record with drill-style 808 slides, or an R&B chord progression under a dark trap drum pattern. Use these as a reference for "what's the DNA of this style," then bend it. The goal isn't to sound like a genre — it's to understand why the genre sounds the way it does, so you can make deliberate choices instead of guesses.

A quick-reference for the tempo, drum feel, and defining characteristics of the genres you'll run into most as a producer. BPM ranges are typical ranges, not hard rules — plenty of great records sit outside them.

Synthwave
80–120
Straight, driving four-on-the-floor or rock-style beat. Gated-reverb snare, analog arpeggiated synths, nostalgic minor-key melodies.

The Trick Explained

HALF-TIME VS. STRAIGHT-TIME

▸
What half-time actually is. A drum pattern where the snare/clap lands only on beat 3 of a 4-beat bar instead of the usual backbeat on 2 AND 4. That single change makes a track programmed at a fast BPM feel like it's moving at roughly half that speed, even though the hi-hats and other top-end elements can keep moving fast. The contrast — quick hats, slow-feeling backbone — is the actual trick, not the tempo number itself.
▸
Genres built on this feel: trap, drill, emo-rap, trap soul, the faster phonk subgenres, and often future bass all use this snare-on-3 pattern. Dubstep is where the modern version of this technique originated, before trap picked it up.
▸
Where lo-fi hip-hop fits. Lo-fi gets grouped with these "slow-feeling" genres, but it's usually not using the half-time illusion at all — it's simply written and played at its true tempo (70–90 BPM) with a normal swung backbeat (snare on 2 & 4). It sounds similarly laid-back, just for a different structural reason.
▸
Straight-time genres (dance-pop, house, pop, drum & bass's breakbeat, reggaeton's dembow) keep the backbeat dense enough that the written tempo and the felt tempo match — there's no illusion of "half speed" to create.
