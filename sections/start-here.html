Foundations
Core Concepts

HOW AUDIO WORKS

▸
dBFS (decibels full scale). The unit of volume in a DAW. 0dBFS is the absolute maximum — anything above clips and distorts. There are two different level targets that get confused: before processing, individual tracks are often gain staged to around -12 to -18dBFS so plugins have clean headroom to work with. After mixing, individual elements typically sit between -6 and -3dBFS with the mix bus peaking around -6dBFS before mastering. Both numbers are correct — they refer to different stages.
▸
Clipping. When audio exceeds 0dBFS, the waveform gets "clipped" — the top of the wave is cut flat. This causes harsh, unwanted distortion. Your meters going into the red = clipping. Keep levels in check before they reach the master.
▸
Frequency. Sound is vibration measured in Hz (hertz). Low Hz = low bass sounds (kick, sub). High Hz = high-pitched sounds (hi-hats, cymbals). The human ear hears roughly 20Hz to 20,000Hz (20kHz). Every instrument occupies a range of frequencies in this spectrum.
▸
Dynamic range. The difference between the quietest and loudest parts of your track. A track with high dynamic range has soft moments and loud moments. Heavy limiting and compression reduces dynamic range — everything becomes more consistently loud, but can start to feel lifeless.
▸
Mono vs. stereo. Mono = one channel of audio (sounds the same from every speaker). Stereo = two channels, left and right, which creates width and space. Most music is stereo but needs to sound good in mono too — phones, Bluetooth speakers, and club systems often sum to mono.
Signal Flow

SIGNAL FLOW BASICS

▸
What is signal flow? The path your audio takes from its source (a synth, a sample) through your mixer, effects, and out to your speakers or export. Understanding signal flow means understanding what is happening to your audio at every stage.
▸
The basic path in FL Studio: Instrument/sample → Mixer insert → Pre-Master → Master. Each stage is a place where you can process the audio. The master is the last stop before the final export.
▸
Insert effects (serial). Plugins placed directly on a mixer track process the full signal one after another in sequence. Everything that comes out gets passed to the next plugin in the chain.
▸
Send effects (parallel). A copy of the signal is sent to a separate return track (reverb, delay, parallel compression). The original dry signal is unchanged — you blend the processed version back in. This is why sends are so useful: you preserve the original while adding the effect.
▸
Why the order of plugins matters. EQ before compression means the compressor reacts to the EQ'd signal. Saturation before a limiter means the limiter sees a smoother, more controlled signal. Always think about what each plugin is receiving, not just what it's doing.
Key Terms

TERMS YOU NEED TO KNOW

▸
Gain staging. Setting healthy volume levels at every stage of your signal chain. The goal is clean headroom before processing (-12 to -18dBFS on raw tracks before plugins), with elements settling at their final mix position (-6 to -3dBFS for most elements) after all processing is applied. Gain staging first, mix balance second — they're two separate steps.
▸
Transient. The initial attack of a sound — the sharp spike at the very start of a kick, snare, or pluck. Transients define the "punch" and "snap" of a sound. Compression can preserve or tame transients depending on how it's set.
▸
Headroom. The space between your current peak level and 0dBFS. Headroom gives your plugins room to work without pushing into clipping. Leaving headroom is one of the most important habits in production.
▸
Wet vs. dry. Dry = the original unprocessed signal. Wet = the processed/effected signal. A reverb at 100% wet means you only hear the reverb, no original sound. Most of the time you want a blend of both.
▸
BPM (beats per minute). The tempo of your track. Most trap and emo-rap sits between 130–160 BPM. A common trick is working at half-time — writing at 75 BPM but setting the project to 150 BPM, so the groove feels slower but the tempo is high.
▸
MIDI vs. audio. MIDI is data — note information (which pitch, when, how loud) that tells an instrument what to play. Audio is the actual recorded sound. Synths and virtual instruments play back MIDI. Samples and recorded sounds are audio. Both live in your DAW but behave differently.
Mindset

BEFORE YOU START

▸
You don't need more plugins. The most common beginner mistake is thinking a new plugin will fix the problem. 90% of professional-sounding beats are made with a handful of tools used well. Learn your tools deeply before adding more.
▸
Reference tracks are not cheating. Every professional producer works with reference tracks — commercial songs they're targeting sonically. Load one into your DAW and compare your mix to it constantly. It's not inspiration, it's calibration.
▸
Finishing beats teaches you more than perfecting them. A beat you finish and move on from teaches you something. A beat you spend 3 weeks tweaking teaches you how to tweak the same beat. Finish things.
▸
Your ears adjust. After 30–60 minutes of working on a beat, your ears have adapted to the sound and you stop hearing it accurately. Take breaks. Come back with fresh ears. The mix you hear after a night's sleep is the honest mix.
FL Studio Basics
The Layout

FL STUDIO OVERVIEW

▸
Channel Rack. Where your instruments and samples live. Each row is one sound — a kick, a snare, a synth. You program patterns here in step sequencer mode, or double-click to open the Piano Roll for more detailed programming.
▸
Piano Roll. The detailed note editor. Click and drag to draw notes, adjust velocity (volume of individual notes), and program melodies, chords, and basslines. This is where most of the musical work happens.
▸
Mixer. Where you control volume, panning, and effects for each sound. Every instrument in the Channel Rack should be routed to its own Mixer insert so you can process it independently. Press F9 to open.
▸
Playlist (Song view). Where you arrange your patterns into a full track — verse, hook, breakdown, outro. Pattern mode lets you loop and build; Song mode is where you arrange. Press F5 to open.
▸
Browser. Where you find samples, presets, and plugins. Press F8 to open. Sample packs you've installed will show up in the packs folder. You can favorite folders you use often for faster access.
Routing

MIXER ROUTING

▸
Route instruments to the Mixer. In the Channel Rack, click the instrument name and look for the mixer track number (top left of the instrument settings). Type the mixer insert number you want it routed to. Every sound should have its own insert.
▸
Master insert. Insert 0 is the Master — the final output. Every other insert eventually routes here. Don't put instruments directly on the master; use individual inserts.
▸
Sends. You can route one mixer insert to another using the send buttons at the bottom of each insert. This is how you set up parallel compression chains and reverb/delay returns. Sends should stay at 100% — use faders for blend control.
▸
Pre-Master insert. Create a mixer insert between your grouped tracks and the Master. Route your drum bus, melodic bus, and bass bus here. This is where you put glue compression and any broad shaping before the master chain.
▸
Sidechain routing. To sidechain one element to another (e.g. kick ducking the bass), route the kick send to the bass insert, then set the bass compressor's sidechain input to that send. The kick's signal triggers compression on the bass without being heard through that route.

Good Habits

FL STUDIO HABITS

▸
Name and color everything. Right-click any mixer insert, pattern, or channel rack row to rename and color it. Future-you will thank you. "Mixer Track 7" tells you nothing at 2am.
▸
Save new versions constantly. Ctrl + Shift + S creates a new numbered save without overwriting the last one. Use it before any major change. Storage is cheap, lost work is not.
▸
Use Pattern mode to write, Song mode to arrange. Write all your musical content (loops, fills, variations) in separate patterns in the Channel Rack. Then arrange those patterns in the Playlist. Don't write everything in one giant pattern.
▸
Keep the Master clean. The Master insert is for your final limiter and very light mastering chain only. Don't put creative effects or heavy processing on the Master — that belongs on individual tracks or the Pre-Master insert.
▸
Export stems regularly. File → Export → Wave, then check "Split mixer tracks." Exporting stems forces you to hear each element in isolation and often reveals problems invisible in the full mix.
Mix Level Reference
Starting Point Targets — Every track is different, use these as reference only

WHERE ELEMENTS SIT IN THE MIX

▸
Kick drum: -6 to -4dBFS peak. The kick is usually the loudest element in a trap or emo-rap beat. It needs to punch through the mix — err toward the louder end of this range. The kick level sets the ceiling everything else references against.
▸
808 / bass: -8 to -6dBFS peak. Sits just below or level with the kick. The kick and 808 share the low end — they need to be balanced relative to each other first. If the 808 is too loud, it overwhelms the kick. Too quiet, it disappears on small speakers.
▸
Snare / clap: -10 to -6dBFS peak. Should hit with authority but sit slightly behind the kick. In trap and emo-rap the snare is often layered and processed heavily — its perceived loudness matters more than the raw level number.
▸
Hi-hats: -18 to -12dBFS peak. Hi-hats sit significantly lower than the kick and snare. They add texture and rhythm but shouldn't compete with the main hits. Open hats can push toward -12dBFS; closed hats and ghost hats sit lower.
▸
Lead melody / main synth: -10 to -6dBFS peak. The lead sits close to the kick in perceived loudness. It's the main musical focal point and needs to be heard clearly without being buried. Don't let it get swallowed by pads or chords.
▸
Pads / chords: -18 to -12dBFS peak. Supporting harmonic elements sit well back in the mix. Their job is to fill space and provide harmonic context — not to be heard as distinct elements. If you can clearly identify the pad as separate from the lead, it's probably too loud.
▸
Arpeggios / counter-melodies: -18 to -14dBFS peak. Secondary melodic elements sit behind the lead. Enough to add texture and movement, not enough to draw focus away from the main hook.
▸
Vocal samples / chops: -12 to -8dBFS peak. Depends entirely on whether the vocal is a lead or a texture. Lead vocal = treat like the melody. Chopped vocal texture = treat like a pad. The distinction changes everything about how it should sit.
▸
Reverb / delay returns: -20 to -14dBFS peak. Effects returns sit well behind the dry signal. The reverb tail should be felt more than heard on most elements. If your reverb return is louder than this, it's probably washing out the clarity of the mix.
▸
Pre-master bus output: -6 to -3dBFS peak. The combined output of everything before it hits the master chain. Leave enough headroom for the master compressor and limiter to work without the input signal already clipping.
⚠ These are starting points, not rules. The right level for any element is wherever it sounds right in the context of the full mix. Use these numbers to get in the ballpark, then trust your ears. A mix that measures perfectly but sounds wrong is still wrong.
Practical Tips

MIXING BY LEVELS

▸
Start with the kick, then the 808. Get these two balanced first — everything else references against them. Most mixing decisions become clearer once the low end foundation is right.
▸
Mix the loudest element first, work downward. Set the kick level, then balance the snare to the kick, then the lead to the snare, then everything else to the lead. Building top-down keeps you from pushing everything too loud trying to make quieter elements audible.
▸
Use a reference track to calibrate. Load a commercial track you're targeting into FL and compare peak levels on your SPAN meter. If their kick peaks at -5dBFS and yours is at -2dBFS, you're already starting with a loudness imbalance before the master chain.
▸
Perceived loudness ≠ peak level. A sustained pad at -14dBFS can feel louder than a transient kick at -6dBFS because of how long it holds energy. Use your ears alongside the meters — they tell different parts of the story.
▸
The low-volume test. Turn the mix way down until it's barely audible. The kick, 808, and lead melody should still be the clearest things you can hear. If something disappears or something else takes over, your level balance needs work.
Gain Staging vs Mixing

TWO STAGES, TWO GOALS

▸
Gain staging (before mixing). The goal is clean, consistent signal levels entering your plugins so they have headroom to work correctly. Aim for individual tracks peaking around -12 to -18dBFS before any processing is applied. This is purely technical — it's not about how things sound relative to each other yet.
▸
Mixing (the actual balance). After gain staging and processing, use your faders to set how elements sit relative to each other. This is where the -6 to -3dBFS targets come into play — it's the finished position of each element after all decisions are made.
▸
Plugins add gain. Saturation, compression with make-up gain, EQ boosts — all of these raise the level of your track. A track gain staged to -16dBFS can easily sit at -6dBFS after processing. That's fine and expected — the gain staging gives the plugins clean signal to work with, the processing shapes it, the fader sets the final position.
▸
Use the fader as your final volume control. Don't use plugin output knobs to set mix levels — use the mixer fader. Output knobs on plugins should only compensate for gain reduction within that plugin. The fader is your mix position.
FL Studio — Tips & Tricks
Piano Roll

PIANO ROLL TRICKS

▸
Stamp tool for chords. In the Piano Roll toolbar, select the Stamp tool (or press Alt+C) and pick a chord shape from the dropdown. Click anywhere on the grid to drop that chord instantly. Fast way to try different voicings without placing notes one by one.
▸
Scale highlighting. Right-click the piano keys on the left of the Piano Roll and select a scale + root note. Notes in the scale highlight in a lighter color. Makes staying in key visual and fast — especially useful when you're learning theory.
▸
Velocity lane. At the bottom of the Piano Roll is the velocity lane — green bars showing the velocity of each note. Click and drag to change individual velocities. Select multiple notes and drag to scale all of them together proportionally.
▸
Ghost notes. When you have multiple patterns on the same channel, you can see notes from other patterns as faded "ghost" notes in the background. Helps you write a harmony or bassline that lines up perfectly with an existing melody without switching windows.
▸
Quick quantize. Select notes and press Ctrl + Q to snap them to the current snap setting. Useful for cleaning up timing after drawing notes freehand. Hold Alt while drawing to bypass snap temporarily for micro-timing.
▸
Note properties panel. Right-click any note to access the note properties. You can set exact pitch, position, length, velocity, and panning for a single note. Useful for surgical adjustments without having to resize or drag.
Mixer

MIXER TRICKS

▸
Sidechain with Peak Controller. A native FL method for sidechaining without a compressor. Add a Peak Controller to the kick's mixer insert, then link it to the volume fader of the 808/bass insert. The kick's peak drives the volume down on the bass automatically. Fast and light on CPU.
▸
Mixer track routing colours. When you select a mixer insert, the tracks it sends to highlight in green and the tracks it receives from highlight in blue. Use this to quickly visualize your routing and catch anything going somewhere unexpected.
▸
Solo with headphone icon. The headphone icon on a mixer insert solos that track. Useful for checking a single element in isolation without muting everything else manually.
▸
Stereo separation knob. On each mixer insert there's a small knob between the pan and the fader. This controls stereo separation — turning it left narrows the stereo image toward mono, turning it right widens it. Useful for keeping the low end of a stereo sample under control without a dedicated plugin.
▸
Effect chain bypass. Click the green power button at the top of the effects chain on any mixer insert to bypass all effects on that track at once. Useful for A/B comparing your processing vs. the dry signal without turning off individual plugins.
▸
Link mixer faders to a controller. Right-click any fader and select Link to Controller to map it to a MIDI knob or fader. Lets you adjust mix levels in real time while the track plays — faster than clicking.
Edison & Sampling

EDISON TIPS

▸
What Edison is. FL Studio's built-in audio recorder and editor. You can record audio directly into Edison, edit it, slice it, reverse it, and send clips back to the playlist or sampler. It's also the easiest way to resample your own beats.
▸
Resampling your beat. Add Edison to the Master insert. Hit record in Edison, then play your beat. Edison captures the full output of your master in real time. Stop recording, drag the captured audio back into the Playlist or into a sampler channel. Now you can chop, pitch, and rearrange your own material.
▸
Reverse audio. Load a sample into Edison, select all (Ctrl + A), then click Reverse (the backwards R button). Drag the reversed version back into the project. Useful for reverse cymbals, reversed stabs, and creating risers from existing sounds.
▸
Noise reduction. Edison has a built-in noise reduction tool. Record a section of pure noise (room tone, hiss), select it, click the noise reduction button to sample the noise profile, then apply it to the full recording. Cleans up lo-fi or recorded audio without a dedicated plugin.
▸
Time-stretching. In Edison, select audio and use the time-stretch function to slow down or speed up audio without changing pitch. Stretch a drum loop to match your tempo, or stretch a sample to create unnatural artifacts for texture.
Workflow

POWER USER HABITS

▸
Template projects. Build a default project with your drum bus, pre-master, and master chain already set up and save it as a template. Every new beat starts from this rather than from scratch. Saves 10–15 minutes per session and keeps your routing consistent.
▸
Fruity Peak Controller for automation. Peak Controller isn't just for sidechaining — you can link it to almost any knob in FL. Link it to a filter cutoff, a reverb wet knob, or a volume fader to create dynamic movement driven by the audio signal rather than drawn automation.
▸
Right-click drag for automation clips. Right-click any knob in the mixer or on a plugin and select "Create automation clip." FL generates an automation lane in the Playlist that you can draw into. Faster than real-time recording automation for precise control.
▸
Pitch automation on 808s. Open the 808's channel settings, right-click the pitch knob, and create an automation clip. Draw pitch slides between bars to make the 808 glide between notes melodically without having to program it in the Piano Roll.
▸
Fruity Formula Controller. A native plugin that lets you write mathematical formulas to control any parameter. Advanced, but lets you create complex modulation shapes (e.g. exponential fade-ins, rhythmic patterns) that aren't possible with standard automation curves.
▸
Use SPAN on the master. SPAN is a free spectrum analyzer (find it in your DAW's plugin browser under Analysis or install it free from Voxengo). Add it to the master insert to see your frequency balance in real time. Compare what you see against a reference track loaded into FL to spot frequency imbalances fast.
▸
Playlist clip colors tell a story. Color your Playlist clips by section — verse in blue, hook in yellow, breakdown in red. At a glance you can see the entire structure of your track and spot arrangement problems (a section that's too long, a missing transition, uneven energy distribution).
Native Plugins Worth Knowing

UNDERRATED NATIVE TOOLS

▸
Fruity Peak Controller. Links audio signal peaks to any parameter. The most versatile native modulation tool FL has. Sidechain compression, filter ducking, tremolo effects — all achievable without third-party plugins once you understand it.
▸
Fruity Stereo Enhancer. Simple stereo width control. More transparent than many third-party wideners for subtle use. Good on pads and chords when you want to push them to the sides without introducing phasing.
▸
Fruity Stereo Shaper. Mid/side processing. Lets you apply different EQ, compression, or effects to the center (mid) and sides independently. Useful on the master for controlling stereo width in specific frequency ranges.
▸
Transient Processor. Found under Dynamics. Lets you boost or cut the attack and sustain of a sound independently. Great for adding snap back to drums after compression has tamed the transients, or reducing the sustain of a boomy kick without EQ.
▸
Effector. Found under Mixing. A multi-effect unit with a 3x3 grid of effects you can touch/click to apply. Chorus, distortion, filter, reverb, and more — all controllable with an XY pad. Good for live-style effect throws and transitions.
▸
Gross Beat. Time and volume manipulation plugin. Lets you create stutters, glitches, tape-stop effects, half-time effects, and rhythmic volume gating using preset patterns. One of the most creative tools in FL for adding movement and variation to loops.
▸
Parametric EQ 2. FL's main EQ. Fully featured with up to 7 bands, spectrum analyzer display, and per-band controls. The right-click menu on each band gives you extra options like stereo/mid-side mode per band. More powerful than it looks at first.
