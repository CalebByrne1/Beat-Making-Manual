Scales & Emotions
Foundation

BUILDING SCALES FROM STEPS

▸
Major scale formula: W–W–H–W–W–W–H (whole and half steps). Sounds bright, resolved, positive.
▸
Natural minor formula: W–H–W–W–H–W–W. Sounds dark, emotional, introspective.
▸
Half step = 1 semitone (adjacent piano keys, including black keys). Whole step = 2 semitones.
▸
Starting point matters. The same 12 notes rearranged around a different root = different key, different emotional character.
▸
Pentatonic shortcut: Minor pentatonic = 1 b3 4 5 b7. Only 5 notes — nearly every combination works. The safest starting scale for writing melodies from scratch.

Emotional Reference

EMOTIONAL CHARACTER GUIDE

▸
Major: Happy, bright, uplifting, resolved. Works for celebratory, energetic, or positive tracks. Often combined with darker production elements for contrast.
▸
Natural Minor: Sad, dark, introspective, emotional. The most common key for trap, emo rap, and most contemporary hip-hop production.
▸
Dorian Mode: Minor with a raised 6th. Less dark than natural minor, has a "cool" quality. Used in jazz and some neo-soul influenced production.
▸
Phrygian Mode: Minor with a lowered 2nd. Very dark, ominous, tense. Common in horror-adjacent production, Spanish-influenced sounds, dark trap.
▸
Mixolydian Mode: Major with a lowered 7th. Slightly ambiguous — bright but with an edge. Used in blues and gospel-influenced production.
▸
Whole Tone Scale: All whole steps. Dreamlike, floating, unresolved. Used for specific psychedelic or surreal moods.
Emotional Reference

SCALE DEGREE EMOTIONAL CHARACTER

▸
1 — Tonic: Home. Resolved, stable, grounded — the note every melody is ultimately trying to get back to, regardless of key.
▸
2 — Supertonic: Mild tension, questioning, anticipatory. A passing note that wants to keep moving rather than sit still.
▸
3 — Mediant: Defines the mood before anything else does. Major 3rd = brightness, minor 3rd = darkness — this single note is what tells your ear "happy" or "sad."
▸
4 — Subdominant: Longing, lifting, builds toward something. Sets up a return to the 5 or the 1 — rarely a place to rest.
▸
5 — Dominant: Strong tension, urgency, wants to resolve back to the 1. The other anchor point besides the root — land here to feel confident and settled.
▸
6 — Submediant: Melancholy, introspection, emotional depth. In minor keys this is often where the emotional peak of a melody sits.
▸
7 — Leading tone: Maximum tension, unstable, pulls hard toward the 1. The shortest distance to "home" — use it right before a resolution, not as a place to rest.
Tension map (low → high pull): 1 and 5 are your anchors — land here to feel resolved and confident. 6 sits in the middle as emotional color. 7 is maximum pull toward the root, the degree that makes a resolution actually feel earned. In B minor: lean on the 6 and 7 for something unresolved and emotional, land on the 1 and 5 for something settled and confident. Same relationships in any key — it's not about the notes, it's about where they sit.
Emotional Reference

MINOR KEY CHORD FINGERPRINTS

▸
i (minor): Dark home base. Settled but heavy — the chord everything else wants to resolve back to.
▸
ii° (diminished): Anxiety, dread, instability. Use sparingly as a passing chord to create unease right before a resolution.
▸
III (major): Bittersweet relief. A major chord borrowed from the relative major — feels like a brief lift out of the minor mood without losing it.
▸
iv (minor): Deep sadness, longing. A softer pull than the v/V — often used to build emotional weight before a hook.
▸
v / V (minor / major): Tension that wants to resolve to i. The minor v feels softer and moodier; the major V (borrowed from harmonic minor) feels more urgent and dramatic.
▸
VI (major): Melancholy but open — often the emotional peak of a progression, especially in i–VI–III–VII style loops.
▸
VII (major): Cinematic lift, dramatic. Feels like a key change is coming even when it isn't — great for building into a hook.
The progression packs people sell are really just curated combinations of these seven chords. Once you know what each one feels like on its own, you can build — or reverse-engineer — any progression by ear instead of buying it.

Music Theory
Foundation

INTERVALS

▸
An interval is the distance between two notes. Intervals have both a number (2nd, 3rd, 5th) and a quality (major, minor, perfect, diminished, augmented).
▸
Perfect 4th (5 semitones): Stable, open. Great for bass movement and power chords.
▸
Perfect 5th (7 semitones): Open, powerful, neutral. Foundation of most chord structures.
▸
Minor 3rd (3 semitones): Slightly dark, emotional. Building block of minor chords.
▸
Major 3rd (4 semitones): Bright, pleasant. Building block of major chords.
▸
Tritone (6 semitones): Dissonant, tense, unresolved. Used for suspense. Always wants to resolve up or down a half step.
▸
Major 7th (11 semitones): Dreamy, jazzy, bittersweet. Adds sophistication to chord progressions when stacked on minor or major triads.
Technique

TENSION & RELEASE

▸
Tension = unresolved energy. Dissonance, unstable chords, unfinished phrases. It makes the listener lean forward, waiting for something.
▸
Release = resolution. Consonance, stable chords, landing on the tonic. The payoff after the tension.
▸
The V–I move is the fundamental tension → release in Western music. The dominant chord (V) creates tension; resolving to the tonic (I) releases it.
▸
Delay the resolution. Let the tension sit longer than expected for more emotional impact when release comes.
▸
Suspended chords (sus2, sus4): Neither major nor minor — tonally ambiguous. Create tension without being harsh. Resolve to major or minor for the payoff.
▸
Apply to melody: A melody that ends on the 7th degree instead of the root leaves tension unresolved. That tension can carry across a phrase break and resolve at the start of the next section.
Advanced

CHORD EXTENSIONS

▸
7ths (maj7, m7, dom7): Adding the 7th degree gives chords color. Minor 7 (m7) = smooth, slightly dark. Major 7 (maj7) = dreamy. Dominant 7 (dom7) = bluesy tension.
▸
9ths: Stacking a 9th (same as a 2nd, one octave up) adds richness without being "jazz-complex." Minor 9 = lush and dark. Major 9 = bright and expansive.
▸
Add9 vs. maj9: Add9 = root + 3rd + 5th + 9th (no 7th). Less dense than full major 9. Good for keeping chords open.
▸
Don't overthink extensions. A standard minor triad with good voicing often sounds better than a complex extended chord voiced poorly. Understand the basics thoroughly before chasing complexity.
Keys & Modes

KEYS & MODES

▸
Relative major/minor: Every major key has a relative minor that shares the same notes (just a different root). C major = A minor. Use this to pivot between feelings without changing notes.
▸
Modes are rotations of the major scale. Start on the 1st degree = Ionian (major). Start on the 6th degree = Aeolian (natural minor). Start on the 2nd = Dorian, 3rd = Phrygian, etc.
▸
Common mode color changes from natural minor: Dorian (raised 6) = smoother. Phrygian (lowered 2) = darker/tenser. Harmonic minor (raised 7) = dramatic, Eastern flavor.
▸
Modal borrowing (borrowed chords): Temporarily using a chord from a parallel mode. E.g., using the bVII chord from natural minor in a major key. Adds unexpected color to a progression.
Synth & Sound Design
Fundamentals

SYNTH FUNDAMENTALS

▸
Oscillator (sound source): Sine = pure, sub. Saw = bright, buzzs. Square = hollow, nasal. Triangle = soft, flute-like. This is where the raw material comes from.
▸
Filter (tone shaping): Low-pass (removes highs) is most common. Cutoff controls where filtering starts. Resonance adds emphasis at the cutoff point — boosts character but can sound harsh high.
▸
Envelope (shape over time): Attack controls fade-in, Decay controls fall to Sustain level, Release controls fade out after key released. The ADSR shapes whether sounds are plucky, droning, or anything between.
▸
LFO (modulation): A slow oscillator that moves another parameter over time. LFO on pitch = vibrato. LFO on filter = auto-wah. LFO on volume = tremolo.
▸
Learn one synth completely before switching. Vital (free) and Serum are both comprehensive — pick one and go deep. Moving between synths before understanding one means you're always starting over.
Producer Textures

PI'ERRE / TM88 TEXTURES

▸
Dreamy pad layers: Soft attack, slow filter opening, long release. Detuned oscillators (2–3 cents) + subtle chorus for width. Low-pass filter with gentle cutoff sweep.
▸
Bright pluck leads: Fast attack + fast decay, minimal sustain. Saw or triangle oscillator. High-pass to thin it, short reverb to space it. Pitch envelope for a slight attack transient.
▸
Flute-style melodies: Sine or triangle base, slight vibrato LFO on pitch (rate: 5–6 Hz, depth: very low). Soft attack. Reverb heavily to float in the mix.
▸
Bell/marimba tones: FM synthesis (op-to-op modulation) or physical-modeling. Fast attack, medium decay, no sustain, no release. Short room reverb.
▸
Texture layers (not melodies): Soft, detuned pads with long attack, wide stereo spread, very quiet in the mix. They fill space without drawing attention. These are what make a beat feel expensive vs. empty.
Advanced

ADVANCED SOUND DESIGN

▸
FM synthesis: One oscillator (modulator) modulates the frequency of another (carrier). High mod ratio = harsh, metallic. Low mod ratio = subtle, bell-like. Velocity controlling mod depth = expressive.
▸
Wavetable scanning: Moving through a wavetable over time (via LFO or envelope) creates evolving, animated textures. Set a slow LFO on the wavetable position for constant movement.
▸
Unison detuning: Multiple oscillator voices slightly detuned apart. 2–4 voices at 5–15 cents detune = wide, warm. Too much = washy and pitch-unstable.
▸
Velocity-to-filter: Map velocity to filter cutoff. Loud notes = brighter. Soft notes = darker. Makes programmed patches feel like they're being played, not triggered.
▸
Layering complementary timbres: Pair a sub-frequency sine with a mid-frequency saw. Each occupies a different frequency zone so they don't fight — together they make one full sound.
Experimental & Try This
Creative Constraints

BREAK THE RULES

▸
Leave the mistake in. If something sounds interesting when something goes wrong, keep it. The accident is often more interesting than the plan.
▸
Work with a single sample. Chop, pitch, reverse, and layer a single drum break or vocal sample into a full beat. Constraints force creative solutions.
▸
Start with the unusual element first. Most producers start with drums. Start with a texture, a melody, or a vocal chop instead and build the drums around that. Forces the groove to serve the sound instead of the other way around.
▸
No quantize. Play a melody in live with no quantization correction. The feel will be different — sometimes better. Use the imperfection as character.
▸
Use a different time signature. 3/4, 5/4, or 7/8 create unusual grooves that standard trap templates don't touch. Even a small section in an odd meter can recontextualize the whole beat.
Rhythm

UNCONVENTIONAL GROOVES

▸
Half-time feel: Kick and snare pattern that implies half the actual BPM. Common in modern trap — the beat is 140 BPM but grooves feel like 70. Hi-hats stay fast, drum hits get sparse.
▸
Polyrhythm: Two rhythms with different cycle lengths played simultaneously. 3 against 2, 4 against 3. Creates a shifting, phase-like feel that doesn't repeat until the cycles align.
▸
Triplet 808 patterns: Quantize your 808 notes to triplets instead of straight 16ths. Changes the feel entirely — slower-sounding, more melodic, harder to predict.
▸
Random-quantize drums: Apply subtle manual nudges to programmed drums (+/- 5–10ms off the grid). Each hit lands slightly before or after the beat. Sounds less robotic, more like a live drummer with feel.
▸
Displaced loops: Take a 4-bar pattern and offset it by 2 bars so the kick falls on unusual parts of the phrase. The groove repeats but at a different starting point relative to the musical phrasing.
Technique

RESAMPLING IDEAS

▸
Resample your own beat. Record 4 bars of your mix, drop it back as a single sample, chop it. You get textures and transitions that weren't possible with the individual elements.
▸
Resample + pitch down for pads. Record a high-frequency sound (hi-hats, bright synth). Pitch it down 1–2 octaves. Often becomes a usable texture or atmosphere that has no obvious relation to its source.
▸
Pitch your reverb tail. Take a reverb return, record it as audio, pitch it up or down. Turns reverb into melodic content or dark ambient texture.
▸
Velocity-chopped vocal samples: Chop a vocal into Edison, map the chops to a sampler with velocity zones. Low velocity triggers one syllable, high velocity another. Creates expressive vocal textures when played on a keyboard.
▸
Record through effects live. Print a sequence through a chain (distortion, reverb, pitch shift) while it plays. The result is committed — no safety net. Forces commitment and creates irreversible character.
Prompts

TRY THIS

▸
Write a melody with only 3 notes. Make it as interesting as possible through rhythm, velocity, and repetition variation.
▸
Make a beat with no kick drum. Let the 808 and hi-hats carry the low and high end alone.
▸
Take a sample you've never used. Find one interesting moment (2–4 bars). Build an entirely new beat around just that moment.
▸
Make a beat at a tempo you'd never normally use — 85 BPM, or 175 BPM. Forcing an unusual tempo forces unusual decisions.
▸
Copy the exact drum pattern from a beat you admire and try to write original chords and melody on top. Separate the groove study from the harmony study.
▸
Finish a beat in one 90-minute session. Set a timer. No revisiting. Export whatever exists at the end. Finishing practice is more important than the result.
Ear Training
Active Listening

CRITICAL LISTENING

▸
Analyze, don't just enjoy. Pick 3–5 reference tracks and listen with intention. What's in the left channel? What's in the right? What frequencies dominate the low end? How long are the reverb tails?
▸
Find the drum pattern first. Listen and tap along to isolate kick, snare, and hi-hat timing separately. Then try to recreate it from memory in your DAW.
▸
Identify the key. Hum the root note of a song you know. Find it on a keyboard. Over time this becomes faster — this is how producers "hear" a key quickly.
▸
Listen to genres you don't produce. House, jazz, classical, soul. Different genres solve the same musical problems (space, tension, energy) in completely different ways. Cross-pollination is underrated.
▸
Recreate a section exactly. Take a 4-bar section of a track you admire and reproduce it note-for-note, drum hit for drum hit. This reveals technical gaps faster than any tutorial.
Pitch

HEARING PITCH

▸
Relative pitch. Recognizing that one note is a 5th above another. This is trainable. Play intervals daily — eventually you stop counting and start hearing the gap as a sound with a character.
▸
Scale degrees by sound. The 7th degree sounds unresolved. The 5th sounds stable. The b3 sounds minor and dark. Learning these associations speeds up melody writing and transposing.
▸
Tune 808s by ear. Play the 808 alongside the key chord and adjust pitch until the beating (warbling) stops. When it disappears, the 808 is tuned. This trains pitch sensitivity faster than any exercise.
▸
Sing before playing. Hum or sing a melody before programming it. The ear leads the fingers. Melodies you sing are usually more natural than melodies you compose by trial and error on a keyboard.
Evaluation

EVALUATING YOUR MIX

▸
A/B against a reference. Match loudness (use SPAN), then switch back and forth every 5–10 seconds. Your ear resets on the switch. Differences in low end, width, and high-end clarity become obvious.
▸
Listen at low volume. If balance disappears at quiet levels, something is wrong with frequency balance or relative levels.
▸
Come back the next day. Fresh ears after sleep are the most reliable judge you have. What seemed finished the night before often reveals obvious issues after rest.
▸
Test on multiple systems. Laptop speakers, phone, headphones, car, earbuds. A mix that sounds great on all of them is done. A mix that only sounds good on studio monitors isn't finished.
▸
Have someone else listen without telling them what it is. Watch their reaction, not their comment. Do they nod? Do they look away? Do they ask you to turn it up? The body language before the opinion matters more.
Workflow & Mindset
FL Studio Workflow

FL WORKFLOW TIPS

▸
Use template projects. Save a project with your standard signal chain, pre-routed mixer tracks, and default instruments set up. Start every session from this template.
▸
Color-code your mixer. Drums = one color, melodic elements = another, effects returns = another. You can navigate a complex mix in seconds by color without reading labels.
▸
Use pattern clips for arrangement. Name each pattern clearly (Verse Drums, Hook Melody, Bridge Pad). In the playlist, you drag and drop labeled clips rather than trying to remember what nameless patterns are.
▸
Render stems early. Before a session gets complex, render finished element groups (drums, melody, bass) as audio stems. Reduces CPU load and simplifies the session.
▸
Leave notes to yourself. FL's label system or even a notepad text file in the project folder. Note the key, BPM, sample sources, and any unfinished ideas. Future-you will thank present-you.
Habits

PRODUCTIVE HABITS

▸
Show up every day, even briefly. 20 minutes of focused work builds the skill faster than one 10-hour session a week. Consistency over intensity.
▸
Separate creation from evaluation. When writing: no second-guessing, no deleting. When mixing/arranging: no new ideas. The creative mode and the critical mode use different thinking — running them simultaneously kills both.
▸
Finish things even when they're not good. A finished mediocre beat teaches more than an abandoned great idea. Finishing is its own skill. Build it deliberately.
▸
Study your influences technically. Don't just listen to producers you admire — pull their beats apart. What key? What drum pattern? How many elements? Where does the melody sit in the frequency spectrum?
▸
Keep an idea log. A running file of melody ideas (recorded on phone), drum patterns, plugin settings worth saving. The idea you dismiss today might be the foundation of something next month.
Perspective

MENTAL MODELS

▸
Constraints are creative tools. "I can only use samples from this pack" or "I can only use 4 instruments" forces invention. Unlimited options cause paralysis.
▸
Compare your work to itself, not to pros. Measuring your work against finished, mastered, mixed commercial releases is unfair to yourself. Compare to your beat from 3 months ago — the growth is what matters.
▸
The music doesn't have to mean anything to you when you make it. It doesn't have to feel meaningful or inspired. Good music is made through process and skill, not just feeling. Show up even when uninspired.
▸
You don't have to be original, you have to be honest. Chasing originality leads to overthinking and self-censorship. Chasing your honest reaction to sound leads to something only you could make.
▸
Volume is the fastest path to skill. Make more beats. Not better beats — more beats. Quality follows quantity once the fundamentals click. Don't agonize over individual beats too long.
